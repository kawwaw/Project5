In this project we investigate the variance of 40 different hands in different configurations. The hands vary in size, shape and configuration. For each hand we have x and y coordinates of 56 specific points of the hand outline. This gives us 112 values for each hand.

To investigate the variation between the hands outline a principal component analysis (PCA) was made. This statistical methods place 40 hands in a 112-dimensional space. This means that all the variables have a dimension orthogonal to all of the others. The 40 points lay as a cloud of points in this multidimensional space, and the analysis finds the direction in the space that represents the largest part of the variance between the 40 points. This direction is called the first principal component (PC1). Next the second direction is found orthogonal to PC1 and describing the largest part of the rest of the variance.

Results

The PCA gives us some remarkable results that we have listed here:

  • As there are only 40 hands in the 112-dimensional space, the first 40 PC´s can capture the entire variation.
  • The first 5-6 PC´s captures almost all variation. The scatterplot view shows that if you change the PC´s shown. For PC´s number 7 and later the points almost have the same values and the variation is very low.
  • The hand outlines on top of each other illustrates that PC1 represent the variation of how spread the fingers are and also the position of the start and end points of the hand outlines.

Conclusion

We can conclude that the PCA is able to find the most important variance between the hands and this variance is easy to interpret visually. Interactive visualization is a great tool to illustrate results of high dimensional data and the analysis of these.
